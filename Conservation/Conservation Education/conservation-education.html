One of the core functions of Community Conservation Programs section is to provide conservation education and extension services to the general populace for their support in wildlife conservation.
Conservation Education Programme aims to instil an appreciation for wildlife, conservation, sustainability, environmental processes, cultural heritage, as well as to equip students with a range of life skills.
As we are now focusing on conservation with people, this contributes to the overall mandate of ZimParks in terms of enhancing wildlife conservation, protection, and management, improving ZimParks brand, establishing linkages, recognition and improvement of relations with stakeholders. Conservation Education is instrumental in soliciting for support from both local and international communities.

World over, challenges facing conservation are on the increase.
Economic hardships parroted by changing lifestyles have led our populaces to be disconnected from matters of conservation of wildlife and its habitats.
To bridge the gap ZimParks has taken the burden upon its shoulders to educate our populace in matters related to wildlife conservation and its habitat.
We say to you students, teachers and community members, a visit to the parks is a learning experience which you need not miss.
ZimParks sees conservation education as a component of the larger umbrella that views wildlife and the environment in its totality.

Conservation education provides authentic opportunities for learning and gives school going children from Primary to tertiary level an opportunity to connect with flora and fauna in a meaningful way.
Zimparks provide students with a real-life context on which to base their learning, and an opportunity to apply their learning to authentic local community opportunities.
Conservation education in schools is about our natural environment and the plants, animals and birds that live in our very own backyards, school playgrounds and protected areas.
Conservation education results in a range of outcomes.
Through students applying acquired knowledge, skills and values, they can actively take a leadership role for a range of real life local conservation issues.

ZimParks do have programs which suits all age groups in our conservation outreach programs.
Changes experienced in the growth of children correlates to their ability to understand and explore the world.
For the effective delivery of conservation education in schools, Zimparks staff uses teaching and learning methods most appropriate for the age and developmental stage of a student.

The focus of conservation education in early childhood should encourage children to explore and experience their local environment through nature- based play.
Teachings graduate with age into participating in local conservation opportunities in home and school life where students can apply their learning and take action for the environment. For adolescence, teachings comes with the application of knowledge, skills and values through participating and collaborating in conservation.

In today’s conservation practises, indigenous communities play a pivotal role in effective landscape scale conservation of wildlife resources.
Community involvement in all activities including planning and natural management to ensure the long-term success of any project critical in the conservation field.
One way to achieve this is through Community conservation education which aims to enhance a community’s wellness through thoughtful environmental action.
It fosters collaborative learning and action, taking into account the social, cultural, economic, and environmental conditions of a community.
Building connections among people is important in achieving conservation objectives.
Local communities play a vital role in the fight against wildlife crime.
However, engaging communities to support wildlife conservation activities is often challenging and in most cases, takes a long time.
ZimParks found it important to build community institutions that represent the voice of the people.
It is important that communities acquire critical skills such as planning, budgeting, financial management, and monitoring to help them engage and lead wildlife related livelihoods project activities and supporting wildlife resources protection.
To achieve this ZWPMA has posted full time Extension and Interpretation Officers in each Province work directly with communities.

Without suitable institutions and know-how to make a living, more and more people are forced engage into illegal activities which impact negatively on wildlife conservation.
Among others encroaching into the protected areas in search of fertile land for crop production.

Limited access to quality education diminishes the opportunities available to young people in these landscapes to set up enterprises which can support livelihoods without diminishing biodiversity integrity.
In our social set up few economic and training options are available for women and the youth to increase their family incomes, driving them to continue to put pressure on natural resources without understanding the potential value and economic potential of biodiversity protection.
Zimbabwe Parks and Wildlife Management Authority is very much concerned about enhancing community capacity to address the issues of poverty.
It seeks to improve the state of life of the low-income sector by creating jobs and business chances, access to wildlife resources, and other forms of assistance through conducting trainings in various fields such as fish farming , bee keeping, wildlife farming, institutional frameworks capacity building.
In all the trainings being done Zimparks carry out needs assessment, set organisational training objectives, create training action plan, implement training initiatives, evaluate and revise the training.
This resulted in success stories being witness around the country.

Wildlife Club is an association of young people who have a commitment to conserve Zimbabwe’s natural heritage and the wise use of environmental resources for the benefit of the present and future generations.
The purpose is to create an inclusive community on all educational institutions in Zimbabwe comprised of future scientists, environmental activists, animal lovers, and any student who has a passion for wildlife and is concerned with the global biodiversity crisis.
These wildlife Clubs are based on the understanding that exposing children to wildlife in an engaging and compelling way will create positive long-term perceptions towards wildlife.

Wildlife clubs are there to provide conservation education to youths and support them through training, information sharing and advocacy of wildlife related issues. Wildlife clubs brings wildlife, wild lands, and sustainable development knowledge to students.
They are non-religious and non-political association that does not discriminate in its membership on the basis of gender, ethnicity, religion or political differences.
The competence based Zimsec curriculum being implemented now comprise of wildlife related issues of which most of the teachers have not appreciated during their training in colleges and introduction of these wildlife clubs help to bridge the gap.
To date ZimParks has established wildlife clubs in more than 200 schools in Zimbabwe positive behaviour changes are being witnessed.

Objectives of Conservation clubs

  1. To provide a wide range of activities and training for young people in order to equip them with as future African wildlife conservation leaders and develop their potential leadership.
  2. To develop young people’s sense of responsibilities towards wildlife and environmental heritage in society and to instil positive values in them.
