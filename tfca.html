Overview Kavango Orange Trans Frontier Conservation Area

Vision

This expansive TFCA is the world’s largest at approximately 520,000km², larger than Germany and Austria combined. Occupying the Okavango and Zambezi river basins, it encompasses areas within the borders of Angola, Botswana, Namibia, Zambia and Zimbabwe.

This also includes 36 formally proclaimed national parks and a host of game reserves, forest reserves, game management areas, and conservation and tourism concession areas designated for use of natural resources KAZA TFCA was legally established by form of a treaty on 18 August 2011.

The TFCA aims to conserve the shared natural resources and cultural heritage of this vast area of southern Africa:

  • to promote and facilitate the development of a complementary and linked network of protected areas that protect wildlife and provide and restore dispersal corridors and migratory routes.
  • to develop the KAZA TFCA into a world-class tourism destination, oﬀering a variety of breath-taking adventures and lavish relaxation;
  • to provide the free and easy movement of tourism across borders;
  • to implement programmes that ensure the sustainable use of natural resources in ways that improve the livelihoods of communities and reduce poverty in the region;
  • to harmonise conservation legislation and natural resource management of the TFCA

KAZA TFCA boasts the highest ppulation of African elephant the whole world. Other major populations of a wide range of species such as buﬀalo, hippopotamus, lion, lechwe, roan, sable, eland, zebra, wildebeest, waterbuck, puku, bushbuck, sitatunga, wild dogs as well as spotted hyena also roam the area.

As can be expected over such a vast area, the plant life is remarkable with about 3,000 species, some 100 of which are endemics and more than 500 species of birds that are characteristic of the southern African savannahs, woodlands and wetlands.

To establish a world-class trans frontier conservation and tourism destination area in the Okavango and Zambezi river basin regions of Angola, Botswana, Namibia, Zambia and Zimbabwe within the context of sustainable development.

Mision

To sustainably manage the Kavango Zambezi ecosystem, its heritage and cultural resources based on best conservation and tourism models for the socio-economic well-being of the communities and other stakeholders in and around the eco-region through harmonization of policies, strategies and practices.

Objectives

The vision is further articulated in a list of twelve objectives in the Treaty that need to be attained for the dream to be realized.
These objectives centre around the following key themes:

Integrated natural resource management and land use planning including issues of wildlife dispersal areas, wildlife migratory patterns and corridors; wildlife protection and law enforcement; human-wildlife conﬂict; community based natural resource management; and ﬁre, ﬁsheries and forestry management.

Tourism development including issues of tourism value chains; facilities and infrastructure; immigration and easy movement of tourists; and tourism product development, especially cross-border products.

Governance including issues of harmonization of policies, legislation and practices; capacity development; information, experience and resource sharing and pooling; healthy economic environment; stakeholder engagement; and research.

Overview Great Limpopo Trans Frontier Conservation Area

Great Limpopo Transfrontier Park (GLTP) joins together some of the most established wildlife areas in southern Africa into a core are of approximately 37,572 km2.

The Transfrontier Park links the Limpopo National Park in Mozambique, Kruger National Park in South Africa, and Gonarezhou National Park, Manjinji Pan Sanctuary and Malipati Safari Area in Zimbabwe, as well as two community owned areas, one which forms part of the Kruger National Park, namely the Makuleke Area and the Sengwe communal land in Zimbabwe.

The Presidents of Mozambique, South Africa, and Zimbabwe signed the international Treaty for the establishment of GLTP on the 9th of December 2002 in Xai Xai, Mozambique. This landmark also provides a platform for the future development and implementation of the second-phase Great Limpopo Transfrontier Conservation Area (GLTFCA), which includes an even wide variety of land uses, varying from communal areas to private reserves, adding up to a conservation area of almost 100,000 km².

Projects

Among the projects that Great Limpopo TFCA is currently pursuing, there is the establishment the Great Limpopo TFCA Secretariat to spearhead a number of programmes and projects that are being carried out in the landscape.

There is also the Wildlife Translocation which is done in order to restock national parks in Mozambique with wildlife from the other two Great Limpopo TFCA partner states.

The establishment of Limpopo Tourism Access Facility which seeks to improve tourist movement between Kruger and Gonarezhou National Parks has seen the governments of South Africa and Zimbabwe working tirelessly through bilateral engagements

Key funding partners that include Peace Parks Foundation and the European Union are channeling both technical and financial support towards the establishment of a temporary scheduled crossing while efforts to have a permanent designated crossing point are being made.

Conservation Efforts

In its efforts to conserve wildlife resources, Great Limpopo TFCA Joint Management Board has been initiated the following;

  • Wildlife Poisoning Baseline Study which led to the development of Wildlife Poisoning Strategy
  • Elephant Management Framework
  • Establishment of Joint Park Management Committees
  • Establishment of Joint Security Plans
  • Establishment of different thematic Working Groups that include; Conservation, Wildlife Management and Veterinary Working Group, Human Wildlife Conflict Working Group, and Security and Wildlife Protection Working Group

Overview Great Mapungubwe Trans Frontier Conservation Area

Greater Mapungubwe Transfrontier conservation Area (GMTFCA) is an initiative involving Botswana, South Africa and Zimbabwe. It was established as Limpopo-Shashe TFCA in 2006 by signing of a Memorandum of Understanding.

GMTFCA is situated at the confluence of the Shashe and Limpopo Rivers and it measures 5909km2 encompassing areas in the three countries.

The GMTFCA includes Botswana’s Northern Tuli Game Reserve, South Africa’s Mapungubwe National Park, World Heritage Site and various privately owned ranches and Zimbabwe’s Tuli Circle Safari Area, Sentinel Ranch, Nottingham Estate, Maramani Communal Land, Machuchuta, River Ranch, Hwali Communal Land and Doddieburn.

Selection of the Greater Mapungubwe TFCA was based on rich biodiversity of the area, its scenic beauty and cultural importance of the archaeological treasures of Mapungubwe.

GMTFCA Draft Treaty

The GMTFCA Partner States are working towards the finalization of the draft Treaty so as to pave a way for its signing.

Transboundary Tourism Events

Nedbank Tour de Tuli

Nedbank Tour de Tuli is a cycling event which is jointly hosted in Greater Mapungubwe TFCA by Zimbabwe, Botswana and South Africa.

The event was launched in 2009 in Greater Mapungubwe TFCA and was endorsed by the GMTFCA Partner Countries as an annual event.

The main aim of the event is to raise funds for conservation education of vulnerable rural children living adjacent to protected areas across the SADC region. The event also serves as a marketing and publicity tool for conservation areas in the TFCA. The 2022 edition of the event will be hosted from 19 to 24 July 2022.

Defender Trophy Challenge

The Defender Trophy challenge is a transboundary 4 x 4 tourism product that was introduced to GMTFCA for the first time in 2019. It is a prestigious family event aimed at Land Rover Defender enthusiasts.

It focuses on educating participants on the history, local Culture and Fauna & Flora of the GMTFCA .

GMTFCA Projects

Herding for Health Programme

Transboundary cattle trespassing has always a serious challenge since the establishment of the Greater Mapungubwe TFCA and the local communities has lost their livestock as they are shot when they cross into Botswana side.

In a bid to curb the cattle encroachment from Zimbabwe to Botswana/South Africa or Vice Versa, Greater Mapungubwe TFCA with the support from Peace Parks Foundation has initiated a program known as Herding for Health (H4H) which is a rural development project aimed at establishing integrated management of livestock and improving rural livelihoods in areas at the wildlife-livestock interface.

Herding for Health Project will be implemented in Maramani Area on the Zimbabwean Component of GMTFCA.
